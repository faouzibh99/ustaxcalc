What is a Mill Rate? Property Tax Millage Explained Simply

Mill rate, millage, assessed value, effective rate — property tax has its own language. Here is every term explained in plain English, with examples anyone can follow.

What is a Mill Rate?

A mill rate (also called millage rate) is the amount of tax charged per $1,000 of assessed property value. The word "mill" comes from the Latin "millesimum" meaning one-thousandth.

Simple formula: Property Tax = (Assessed Value / 1,000) × Mill Rate

Example: Your home has an assessed value of $200,000. Your local mill rate is 15. Your tax = (200,000 / 1,000) × 15 = $3,000 per year.

Mill Rate vs Effective Tax Rate

These two terms cause a lot of confusion. Here is the difference:

  • Mill rate — applied to assessed value (which may be only a fraction of market value)
  • Effective tax rate — the actual percentage of your home's market value that you pay in taxes

A state might have a mill rate of 20 but an effective rate of only 0.8% because homes are assessed at 40% of market value. When comparing states, always use the effective rate.

What is Assessed Value?

Assessed value is what your county thinks your home is worth for tax purposes — and it is often NOT the same as market value. States apply an "assessment ratio" to market value to get assessed value.

  • Arizona — assesses at 10% of market value
  • Louisiana — assesses at 10% of market value
  • Most states — assess at 100% of estimated market value

What is a Homestead Exemption? (Quick Definition)

A fixed dollar amount subtracted from your assessed value before your mill rate is applied. Reduces your taxable value and therefore your bill.

What is an Assessment Ratio?

The percentage of your home's market value used to calculate your assessed value. A home worth $300,000 in a state with a 50% assessment ratio has an assessed value of $150,000.

What is a Circuit Breaker?

A program that caps your property tax as a percentage of your income. When your tax bill exceeds the cap, the excess is refunded or credited. Designed to protect low-income homeowners and seniors from being taxed out of their homes.
