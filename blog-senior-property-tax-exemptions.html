Senior Property Tax Exemptions: Every State Program Explained

If you are 65 or older, you may qualify for significant property tax relief. Almost every US state offers exemptions, freezes, or circuit breakers — and most seniors never claim them.

The Three Types of Senior Property Tax Relief

1. Exemptions (Reduce Your Taxable Value)

An exemption removes a set dollar amount from your assessed value before your tax rate is applied. For example, Texas offers an additional $10,000 school tax exemption for homeowners 65+, on top of the standard $100,000 homestead exemption.

2. Assessment Freezes (Lock Your Value)

A freeze locks your assessed value at its current level — so even as your home's market value rises, your tax bill stays the same. Texas, Florida, and many other states offer this for seniors 65+.

3. Circuit Breakers (Cap Tax as % of Income)

Circuit breakers cap your property tax as a percentage of your income. If your tax bill exceeds the cap, the state refunds or credits the difference. Missouri, Illinois, and Michigan have strong circuit breaker programs.

📋 Action step: Contact your county assessor's office and ask specifically: "What senior property tax relief programs am I eligible for?" Many programs go unclaimed simply because homeowners do not know to ask.

Best Senior Programs by State

  • Texas — $10,000 additional exemption + assessment freeze at 65. One of the best in the nation.
  • Florida — Additional $50,000 exemption for low-income seniors 65+. Save Our Homes cap applies.
  • California — Homestead exemption up to $300,000 for qualifying seniors. Prop 19 allows transfer of low assessment to new home.
  • New York — Enhanced STAR program saves seniors up to $3,100/year on school taxes.
  • Illinois — Senior Freeze locks EAV. Senior Citizen Homestead Exemption provides additional $8,000 off EAV.
  • Alaska — Senior exemption of $150,000 off assessed value. Significant savings in any borough.
  • Kentucky — $46,350 homestead exemption for seniors and disabled in 2026.

How to Apply

  1. Call or visit your county assessor or property appraiser's office
  2. Ask for the senior/elderly exemption application form
  3. Bring proof of age (driver's license or passport) and proof of residency
  4. Most deadlines fall between January and April — apply early
