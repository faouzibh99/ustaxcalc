How to Appeal Your Property Tax Assessment and Win

Between 30 and 60 percent of US properties are over-assessed. Yet fewer than 5 percent of homeowners ever appeal. Here is a step-by-step guide to challenging your assessment — and winning.

Why You Should Consider Appealing

Your property tax bill is based on your county assessor's opinion of what your home is worth. That opinion is often wrong — and when it's too high, you overpay every single year until you appeal. The process is simpler than most people think, usually free, and success rates are high for well-prepared homeowners.

Step 1: Get Your Assessment Notice

When your county sends your annual assessment notice, do not file it away. This is the starting point. It shows your assessed value, the tax rate applied, and your final bill. You have a limited window to appeal — usually 30 to 90 days from the notice date.

Step 2: Check Your Property Record for Errors

Visit your county assessor's website and pull up your property record card. Check every detail: square footage, number of bedrooms and bathrooms, lot size, year built, garage, and any special features. Errors are extremely common. If your record shows a finished basement you don't have, or 3 bathrooms instead of 2, you have grounds for a straightforward appeal.

🔍 Real example: A homeowner in Ohio found her property record listed 2,400 sq ft when her home was actually 1,950 sq ft. She appealed, the assessor corrected the record, and her annual tax bill dropped by $680.

Step 3: Find Comparable Sales (Comps)

Look up recent sales of similar homes in your neighborhood — same size, age, condition, and location. If comparable homes sold for less than your assessed value, that is your evidence. Use Zillow, Redfin, or your county's own sales database.

Step 4: File Your Appeal

Most counties allow you to file online. Submit your evidence: comparable sales, photos of your property's condition, any appraisal you have. Be factual and specific. State clearly: "My assessed value is $X, but comparable properties sold for $Y."

Step 5: Attend Your Hearing

Many appeals are resolved informally before a formal hearing. If yours goes to a hearing board, present your evidence calmly and let the numbers do the talking. You do not need a lawyer for most residential appeals.

What Happens If You Win?

Your assessed value is reduced, and your tax bill drops — sometimes retroactively for the current year. The new lower assessment often carries forward to future years as well.
